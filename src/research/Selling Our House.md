> What is the best way to sell our house in Exeter, NH so it's as easy as possible and we maximize the amount of money we keep.

Here are the key strategies to sell your Exeter house with maximum ease and profit retention:

**Pricing Strategy** Get a comparative market analysis (CMA) from a local agent familiar with Exeter's market. Price competitively from the start - overpricing typically leads to longer market time and ultimately lower net proceeds. In New Hampshire's market, well-priced homes often receive multiple offers.

**Preparation and Presentation** Invest in cost-effective improvements that boost appeal: fresh paint, professional cleaning, decluttering, and basic staging. For Exeter specifically, highlight features buyers in that area value like proximity to good schools, Portsmouth access, and any outdoor amenities.

**Selling Method Options**

- **Traditional agent**: Typically 5-6% commission but handles everything and often nets higher sale prices
- **Discount brokers**: 1-3% commission but you handle more tasks
- **For Sale By Owner (FSBO)**: No commission but requires significant time investment and market knowledge
- **iBuyers/cash buyers**: Quick but typically 10-15% below market value

**Timing and Marketing** Spring and early summer are typically strongest in New Hampshire. Ensure professional photos and broad MLS exposure. Given Exeter's proximity to the seacoast, emphasize lifestyle benefits.

**Negotiation Strategy** Don't just focus on price - consider net proceeds after closing costs, timing flexibility, and contingencies. Sometimes a slightly lower offer with fewer contingencies nets more money.

**Cost Management** Budget for typical seller costs: agent commission, attorney fees (required in NH), title insurance, potential repairs from inspection, and moving expenses.

> What are the things we can do to help sell the house that get the best payback? We don't want to spend money on things that will not help sell.

Here are the home improvements with the best return on investment for selling, ranked by cost-effectiveness:

**Highest ROI Improvements (Often 100%+ return)**

- **Deep cleaning and decluttering**: Professional cleaning costs $200-500 but makes rooms appear larger and well-maintained
- **Fresh paint**: Neutral colors like warm whites or light grays. Interior painting costs $1-3 per sq ft but can yield 50-100% return
- **Curb appeal basics**: Landscaping cleanup, mulching, power washing exterior. Usually $300-800 total
- **Minor kitchen updates**: New cabinet hardware, faucet, lighting fixtures. $200-800 investment

**Good ROI Improvements (50-80% return)**

- **Bathroom refresh**: New vanity top, updated fixtures, re-caulking. $500-2000 depending on scope
- **Flooring touch-ups**: Professional carpet cleaning ($100-300) or refinishing hardwood scratches
- **Light fixture updates**: Modern fixtures make spaces feel updated. $100-500 per room

**Avoid These Lower-Return Projects**

- Major kitchen renovations (30-50% return)
- Bathroom gut renovations
- New flooring throughout (unless severely damaged)
- Swimming pools or hot tubs
- High-end appliances
- Custom built-ins

**Staging Considerations** Professional staging costs $1500-3000 monthly but can reduce time on market and increase offers by 1-5%. For Exeter's market, even basic staging helps buyers envision themselves in the space.

**Quick Assessment Test** For any potential improvement, ask: "Will this fix something that would cause a buyer to walk away or negotiate hard?" If yes, it's worth doing. If it's just an upgrade, probably skip it.

The key is making your house feel clean, bright, and move-in ready rather than extensively renovated.

> Our lawn is very patchy and unattractive. Would fixing that help and if so what would you suggest? We have about 2 acres.

For 2 acres, a patchy lawn is definitely worth addressing - it's one of the first things buyers notice and can significantly impact curb appeal and perceived property value.

**Cost-Effective Lawn Solutions for Selling**

**Quick Fixes (4-8 weeks, moderate cost)**

- **Overseeding and fertilizing**: $300-600 for 2 acres. Fall is ideal in NH, but spring works too
- **Strategic sod placement**: Focus only on highly visible areas (front yard, main entrance areas). $1-2 per sq ft
- **Professional lawn treatment**: Hire a service like TruGreen for $100-200/month to get it green and healthy

**Budget-Friendly Approaches**

- **Seed and pray method**: Overseed patchy areas, apply starter fertilizer, keep watered. $200-400 total
- **Focus on the "money areas"**: Just fix the front 1/4 acre that buyers see first
- **Mulched landscaping**: Convert some lawn areas to attractive mulched beds with shrubs. Often cheaper than lawn repair

**What NOT to Do** Don't install expensive sod across the entire 2 acres ($3,000-6,000) - the ROI won't justify it for a sale.

**New Hampshire Timing** Early fall (September) is optimal for seeding in your climate, but if you're selling soon, spring seeding with good watering can work.

**Alternative Strategy** If the lawn is severely damaged, consider marketing it as "opportunity for buyer to customize landscaping" and price accordingly. Some buyers actually prefer a blank slate.

Given your 2-acre lot size, focus your lawn budget on the most visible 1/2 to 3/4 acre and let the buyer handle the rest.

> Please tell me more about over seeding and fertilizing. Our soil is very sandy and lacking in nutrients and organic material.

Sandy, nutrient-poor soil is actually common in the New Hampshire seacoast area, including Exeter. Here's how to overseed effectively for your conditions:

**Soil Preparation for Sandy Soil**

- **Soil test first**: UNH Cooperative Extension offers tests for $15-20 to determine exact nutrient needs and pH
- **Add organic matter**: Spread 1/4 to 1/2 inch of compost or peat moss over seeding areas. For 2 acres, this costs $400-800 but is crucial for sandy soil
- **Consider topdressing**: Mix compost with quality topsoil for better seed-to-soil contact

**Seed Selection for Sandy NH Soil**

- **Tall fescue blends**: Drought-tolerant, deep roots, handles sandy conditions well
- **Fine fescue**: Thrives in poor soil, low maintenance once established
- **Perennial ryegrass mix**: Quick germination, good for overseeding
- Expect to use 3-5 lbs per 1,000 sq ft for overseeding (vs 1-2 lbs for thick lawns)

**Fertilizer Strategy**

- **Starter fertilizer**: High phosphorus (like 18-24-12) at seeding time
- **Slow-release nitrogen**: Sandy soil drains quickly, so slow-release prevents nutrient washout
- **Organic options**: Milorganite or similar - feeds longer in sandy conditions

**Watering Sandy Soil**

- Water more frequently but for shorter periods (sandy soil drains fast)
- Expect 2-3 times daily light watering for first 2-3 weeks
- Consider irrigation timer investment if selling in spring/summer

**Budget Estimate for 2 Acres**

- Seed: $200-400
- Compost/organic matter: $400-800
- Fertilizer: $150-300
- Total: $750-1,500

The organic matter addition is key - without it, seeds may germinate but struggle in pure sand.

> What are the things buyers look for in kitchens as must have items? Our kitchen is at least 20 years old.

For a 20-year-old kitchen, buyers generally expect certain functional basics rather than high-end features. Here's what matters most:

**Must-Have Functional Elements**

- **Working appliances**: All major appliances should function properly - non-working items are immediate red flags
- **Adequate counter space**: Buyers need prep space; cramped counters feel limiting
- **Sufficient storage**: Enough cabinets and drawers for typical household needs
- **Good lighting**: Under-cabinet lighting or pendant lights make cooking areas functional
- **Clean, intact surfaces**: Counters, backsplash, and floors should be in good repair

**Deal-Breakers to Address**

- **Broken cabinet doors/drawers** - cheap fix but looks terrible
- **Severely outdated electrical** - exposed wiring, insufficient outlets
- **Plumbing issues** - leaky faucets, poor water pressure
- **Stained or damaged countertops** - especially if chipped or burned
- **Non-functional exhaust fan** - buyers worry about cooking odors

**Nice-to-Have (But Not Essential)**

- Granite/quartz counters
- Stainless steel appliances
- Large island
- High-end finishes

**Cost-Effective Updates for 20-Year-Old Kitchen**

- New cabinet hardware: $100-300
- Fresh paint on walls and cabinets: $200-500
- Updated light fixtures: $200-600
- New faucet: $150-400
- Clean/repair existing appliances rather than replacing

**When NOT to Renovate** If your kitchen is clean, functional, and the appliances work, many buyers today are happy to update it themselves after purchase. A $20,000 kitchen renovation typically only adds $10,000-15,000 to sale price.

The key is "clean and functional" rather than "updated and trendy."

> Would it be worth replacing cabinet doors so they're not white laminate?

For a 20-year-old kitchen, replacing white laminate cabinet doors can be worth it, but it depends on their condition and your budget.

**When Cabinet Door Replacement Makes Sense**

- **If doors are damaged**: Chipped, peeling, or warped laminate looks cheap and dated
- **If they're yellowed**: Old white laminate often turns yellowish, which screams "dated"
- **If hardware holes are stripped**: Multiple repairs make doors look shabby

**Cost-Effective Options**

- **Refacing**: New doors/drawer fronts on existing boxes: $3,000-6,000 for average kitchen
- **RTF (Rigid Thermofoil) doors**: Clean, modern look, $50-100 per door
- **Shaker-style wood doors**: Timeless style, $75-150 per door
- **Paint existing doors**: If laminate is in good shape, sometimes professional painting works ($800-1,500)

**ROI Reality Check** Cabinet door replacement typically returns 60-80% of cost when selling. For a kitchen with 20-25 doors/drawers, you're looking at $2,500-4,000 investment.

**Alternative Approach** If your white laminate is clean and intact (not yellowed or damaged), consider keeping it and emphasizing the "blank canvas" aspect to buyers. Many prefer to customize themselves.

**Quick Test** Take a close photo of your cabinets and show it to a few friends. If their first reaction is "those look dated," it's probably worth updating. If they say "those are fine," focus your money elsewhere.

For Exeter's market, buyers often expect move-in ready condition, so if the cabinets look obviously dated, the investment could be worthwhile.
